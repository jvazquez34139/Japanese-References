Introduction

私は シラス です( watashi wa shiraso desu ) There are a few things I want to explain about this page. Starting with the main purpose. I want to help give some guidance to anyone interested in learning how to speak and read Japanese as a self-taught learner. I went through several free and paid sources and services to know which are appropriate during any level of the learners' journey. A few of these would have saved me lots of time had I known about them from the beginning. This is the main reason for this article. I want to save new self-taught students a lot of time in their endeavors by pointing them in a somewhat organized approach for tackling the different obstacles faced when learning Japanese from zero experience.


I started from 0 knowledge since I didn't have the option to learn this language in school but I was very interested in several things about it. About Japan as a country in general. The anime, food, and culture all together make learning Japanese so interesting. Knowing the language could even land me a job overseas one day.


Now to explain how this page works.

- I have to say that I am not sponsored or paid to advertise any of these resources. This is all entirely just my honest opinion of these resources. Nor am I making any claim that anyone can self teach themselves using any of the methods mentioned on this page. Nor claiming that the resources I mentioned are the best suited for everybody's specific learning styles. It will all depend on how much time is put into the learning, focus, and determination of the reader.


- I will mention resources such as physical books, android apps, webpages, and youtube channels, and I will talk about what comes included in free and paid packages. Screenshots will also be provided.


- You don't need to read through the whole article. If you Have a preferred platform or method of learning you can skip to the section using the navigation bar. Most of the resources will have just about anything you need to get started and to learn the language if you put enough time and effort.

- I will also give my thoughts of what certain apps or resources could have had to make learning more efficient for the user/reader.


- There will be some gray highlighted Japanese terms or phrases, that by hovering over, you can read the English meaning of.


Lot's of tips!

There are three scripts to the Japanese language and you'll most likely see a combination of the three in everyday text or signs in Japan. The three are Hiragana, Katakana, and Kanji. A lot of references will tell you to start with Hiragana. I agree with this and believe it's the best first step to learning the language. Learning the characters (also referred to as kana) of the hiragana script is will allow you to read most Japanese texts. You don't have to prioritize Katakana as much since it shares most of the same sounds. The characters are also very similar to hiragana so learning hiragana will help you halfway through learning katakana. The purpose of katakana is for spelling words of foreign origin. Ex: ハンバーガー( Hanbāgā ). Kanji are characters from Chinese origin that the Japanese language has adopted during its development. After learning hiragana to where you can begin to read things at about a 2nd or 3rd-grade level I recommend studying kanji. Kanji are not just characters like hiragana and katakana. Each kanji or group of kanji represent a whole word or phrase. They're used to make texts more concise although the readings of all kanji can be written fully in hiragana. 犬( inu ) is the kanji for いぬ( inu ). It's fine if you don't learn the readings for the kanji right away. I found it more effective to learn the meanings first and then learn the readings after you learned a few dozen kanji. If you're finding it hard to stay focused, or if you feel you're not making enough progress, then just keep in mind that it is a whole language with a completely new way of saying and reading the thousands of words you've already learned and practiced since the beginning of your life. It's not gonna happen overnight. Even just learning a new phrase or about 5 new words a day is very good progress. The most important thing is to remember to always try to find some way to use what you learned in your everyday life so you can retain it better. What I do is for 20 minutes a day I practice all the kanji I know or practice hiragana. Just using what you know for 20 minutes a day will keep it cycling through your neurons and it won't fade away. The best thing you can do is move to japan for a few years if you could afford it. learn phrases that are relevant to your everyday situations or show up in anime or manga often. This is best with dubbed anime. The last tip for the study is to try as hard as you can to not rely on looking things up you don't remember when you're practicing. Sure it's fine when you're learning it for the first time, but when it comes to practicing nothing helps more than forcing yourself to recall it on your own. The more you try hard at recalling the thing you learned the more you're brain bumps it up to a higher priority bin in your memories storage. It thinks if you're trying that hard to remember it then it must be worth knowing and it'll keep it at an easier access area for when you do need to use it. This may be a very unpopular opinion but I think it's best to skip the practice on writing japanese. Most do say it's import to immerse yourself as much as possible into the language but writing will only slow down your progress on learning more kanji and the grammar. In most settings you wont need to write a physical letter and it takes more time to practice writing than just learning to read it. Most of the time you'll just be reading it or typing it.


Summary

  • - Start with Hiragana. Everything else depends on this!
  • - As soon as you can start practing as many kanji as you can!
  • - Don't lose motivation, it's worth it. And it will take a while!
  • - Force your mind to recall when studying. Rev up your neurons!
  • - Try to use what you learn or practice it every day!
  • - Writing is outdated. Skip it, learn more!

Apps You Should Try

For Android


Duo Lingo

This app is probably the best known. It offers a smooth learning curve. A beginner can pick it up and learn a few phrases in the first few days of using the app. For free it teaches the writing all three of the Japanese writing scripts. It slowly introduces everyday phrases and words as you learn basic grammar structures. It offers a competitive online environment via a leader board and a ranking system among fellow students of the same study tier as yourself. It's one way it uses to motivate students to put in time into playing more. It has full functionality in the free package. The only thing you can purchase are gems that are used for Duos costumes/skins and for buying streak freezes since streaks are part of the badge/achievement system. Gems can also purchase a life refill but the app lets you take a free practice quiz that grants you back 1 life. There's no limit to how many times you can do the practice test but it won't offer anything new from what you've already learned in your previous lessons.

What I didn't like about the app was that it jumps around too much between learning hiragana to katana to full kanji and phrases. This might not entirely be a bad thing as I know learning one part of a language in full can get boring and tiring sometimes and for some, this bits approach is a nice smooth way for getting a taste for everything and getting a faster feel for how all of it works together in small everyday situations. Duo lingo does seem to progressively increase grammatical complexity as you move on to more advanced topics.


Kanji Tree

Fully functional with just the free package. It has a 3 game modes. The first one is recognition. Recognition focuses on teaching you the meanings to the kanji. The second is readings which focuses on the main readings for the kanji but this instead of multiple choice is more like a virtual flash card and based on the honor system of whether you did know the answer or not. The third is Writing practice. It let's the user pick any list ranging from 60-?000 kanji. It also gives the user two main settings which are Learn and Revize. Learn will prioritize giving you the kanji you havent seen first and move any already learned to the end of the list. With a varying random factor that is also adjustable by the user. The Revize setting gives the user the kanji they've seen already first, but prioritizing the one's that need most work. Setting the random variable to 0 will keep them in order from least known to most known at the end of the list. It also gives the kanji you learned a score of 0-10 and it goes up the more you get them right in multiple choice format as well as brings scores down if you get them wrong. It has a very good tutorial, and works 100% offline. It also contains a dictionary.

My only complaints about this app is that it didnt offer more practice options such as typing in the answer instead of only multiple choice. I felt that each time i would get stuck on a kanji I was practicing it was just too easy for me to look at the options and get it correct by just process of elimination. I don't feel the learning hit as hard when The answer is one of just 4 options. A typed response game mode would have been great. Also it would be nice to have the option of making the users multiple choice options the that are most similar to the correct answer in kanji.
